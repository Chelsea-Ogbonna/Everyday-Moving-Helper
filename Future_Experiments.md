# Future Experiments

## Effect of Cargo Weight on Stability
The current experiments were conducted without evaluating how different cargo weights influence platform performance. Future testing should investigate the effects of carrying light, medium, and heavy loads on vibration levels, force fluctuations, and overall stability. Increasing payload mass may alter the center of gravity and increase the likelihood of tipping or cargo shifting, particularly on rough terrain. Understanding these effects will help establish safe operating limits for the platform.


## Expandable Platform Configuration Testing
A key feature of the Everyday Moving Helper is its adjustable footprint. Future experiments should compare the stability of the platform in fully collapsed, partially expanded, and fully expanded configurations. Force sensor and IMU measurements can be used to determine whether platform expansion affects vibration transmission, maneuverability, or resistance to tipping when transporting large objects.


## User-Control and Navigation Testing
Future studies should evaluate how effectively users can operate the platform while transporting cargo. Metrics such as maneuverability, turning radius, obstacle avoidance, and user workload could be assessed under both remote-control and autonomous-following modes. These tests would provide insight into the overall usability of the system.


## Suspension and Vibration Damping Evaluation
The terrain analysis demonstrated that rough surfaces generate significant vibrations and force fluctuations. Future experiments should investigate the effectiveness of suspension systems, vibration-damping materials, or larger wheel designs in reducing these disturbances. Performance can be evaluated by comparing force sensor and IMU measurements before and after implementing vibration-mitigation strategies.


## Comparative Benchmark Testing
To evaluate the competitiveness of the Everyday Moving Helper, future studies should compare its performance against existing cargo transportation solutions such as conventional carts, wagons, and robotic cargo assistants. Metrics such as stability, cargo capacity, maneuverability, and user effort could be used to quantify performance advantages and identify areas for improvement.
